## STEP 3 — Application Info: A-Frame WebAR

### What is A-Frame?

**A-Frame** is a free, open-source web framework created by Mozilla that allows developers to build Virtual Reality (VR) and Augmented Reality (AR) experiences directly inside a web browser, using standard HTML code. It is built on top of Three.js (a 3D rendering library) and WebXR, but hides all the technical complexity behind simple HTML tags like `<a-scene>`, `<a-sky>`, and `<a-image>`.

### How Does It Work?

When a user opens an A-Frame page on their phone or computer, the browser renders an interactive 3D scene without needing any additional app or plugin. The device's gyroscope and touch controls allow the user to look around a 360° environment by simply moving the phone. When a user points at — or taps — a floating icon (hotspot) in the scene, JavaScript code triggers a response, such as showing an information card.

For this project, A-Frame renders a 360° panoramic photo as a virtual "sky sphere," places interactive hotspot icons at specific 3D coordinates inside that sphere, and plays a looping ambient audio track to reinforce the immersive atmosphere.

### Pros and Cons

| ✅ Advantages | ⚠️ Disadvantages |
|---|---|
| **No app installation required.** Users only need a browser and a URL or QR code. | **High data usage.** A single 360° image can be 2–5 MB; audio adds another 2–3 MB. This can be slow on weak mobile data connections. |
| **Cross-platform.** Works on Android, iOS, desktops, and even in Google Cardboard VR headsets. | **Limited AR tracking.** True AR (overlaying 3D objects on a live camera feed) requires additional libraries like AR.js; A-Frame alone creates VR/360° experiences, not full markerless AR. |
| **Easy to share and embed.** The experience lives at a simple URL that can be embedded in Google Sites, shared via QR code, or opened in any browser. | **Performance varies by device.** Older or low-end smartphones may struggle with large textures, causing lag or crashes. |
| **Free and open-source.** No licensing costs; the community provides thousands of ready-made components. | **Autoplay audio restrictions.** Browsers block audio until the user makes a first interaction (tap or click), which adds a small friction step to the experience. |
| **Rapid development.** A working 360° scene with hotspots can be built in a few hours using HTML and basic JavaScript. | **Not offline by default.** Unless a Progressive Web App (PWA) strategy is added, the experience requires an internet connection. |

## STEP 4 — Description: How WebAR Enhances Film Studies

WebAR technology, specifically through frameworks like A-Frame, opens a powerful new dimension in the study of cinema by allowing students to step *into* the world of a film rather than simply watching it on a flat screen. When a student scans a QR code and is suddenly surrounded by the sounds and visual textures of the Colombian Amazon or the colonial streets of old Bogotá, their brain forms a much deeper emotional and spatial connection to the material being studied. This kind of embodied learning activates multiple senses at once — sight, hearing, and a sense of physical presence — which research in educational psychology consistently links to stronger memory retention and critical engagement. Furthermore, interactive hotspots transform passive viewing into active inquiry: students become explorers who choose which historical or production detail to investigate next, developing agency in their own learning process. The 360° format also helps students understand how a director's choice of location, lighting, and sound design shapes the emotional tone of a scene, making abstract concepts from film theory suddenly visible and felt. Because the experience is accessed through any smartphone browser with no installation required, it is also highly accessible, removing the economic and technological barriers that more expensive AR or VR hardware would impose. In this way, WebAR bridges the gap between academic film analysis and experiential, student-centered learning.

## STEP 5 — Thematic Integration: The Two Films in 360°

### *El abrazo de la serpiente* — Hearing the Amazon

One of the most powerful artistic choices in Ciro Guerra's film is the contrast between silence and the overwhelming, living noise of the jungle. When students are placed inside the 360° Amazon environment — surrounded by bird calls, insects, and the low rumble of the river — they begin to understand *why* the jungle itself functions as a character in the film. The visual and sonic immersion makes tangible what Guerra expresses cinematically: the jungle is alive, ancient, and indifferent to human ambition. Hearing this environment also helps students grasp the themes of colonialism more concretely. The sounds of nature that Karamakate knows intimately are the same sounds that the European explorers attempted to dominate and exploit. By *being* in that space, even virtually, students can reflect on what it means to enter a territory that does not belong to you — a central moral question of the film.

### *La estrategia del caracol* — Seeing the Inquilinato

For Sergio Cabrera's film, the 360° experience of a colonial Bogotá courtyard gives students a spatial and social understanding of the *inquilinato* that a flat-screen viewing cannot fully provide. Standing virtually inside a shared courtyard surrounded by worn plaster walls, laundry lines, and the sounds of a busy city street immediately communicates the density, intimacy, and fragility of the lives depicted in the film. Students understand not just intellectually but *sensorially* why these neighbors are so tightly bound together — they share every inch of space, every sound, every problem. This spatial empathy is essential for appreciating the film's political message: the house is not just property to be disputed in a courtroom; it is an entire social world, a community, an identity. The 360° perspective also allows students to notice architectural details — arched doorways, high ceilings, tiled floors — that place the story specifically within Bogotá's class history, turning the environment itself into a text to be read alongside the film.
